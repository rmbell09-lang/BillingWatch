Stripe Billing Bugs That Cost SaaS Companies Thousands

📅 April 2, 2026 · ⏱ 7 min read

stripe saas billing open-source

Billing bugs are the silent revenue killers in SaaS. They're often invisible — no alarms go off, no dashboards light up red — but their impact accumulates fast: lost revenue, chargebacks, frustrated customers, and a reputation that takes months to rebuild.

This post covers five billing bugs that show up repeatedly in Stripe-powered SaaS companies, why they happen, and how much they can actually cost you.

Bug #1: Phantom Subscriptions

What is it?

A phantom subscription occurs when Stripe incorrectly assigns an existing customer to a new plan or product — often without their knowledge or consent. The customer ends up being charged for something they never signed up for.

How does it happen?

  • Incorrect use of invoice_settings->default_payment_method
  • Misconfigured product and price settings during plan migrations
  • Inadequate validation when creating new plans or products
Real-dollar impact: A company with 100 customers accidentally assigns 20 to a $500/month premium plan during a migration. That's $10,000 in erroneous charges per month — and chargebacks if customers dispute it.

Bug #2: Price Drift on Plan Changes

What is it?

Price drift occurs when Stripe charges a customer an outdated price after a plan change — either too much or too little. Both are bad: overcharging triggers disputes, undercharging erodes margins.

How does it happen?

  • Inadequate use of stripe_price vs. stripe_plan (legacy) settings
  • Failure to propagate pricing metadata across plan transitions
  • Discount logic that doesn't account for plan upgrades correctly
Real-dollar impact: 50 customers on a plan with a 10% discount that wasn't applied correctly. Over a month: $2,500 in overcharges — enough to trigger a wave of disputes and refund requests.

Bug #3: Duplicate Charge Events

What is it?

Duplicate charge events happen when Stripe generates multiple invoices or charges for the same billing period. Customers get billed twice — and they notice.

How does it happen?

  • Incorrect configuration of invoice_settings->tax_rates
  • Webhook handlers that aren't idempotent — processing the same event twice
  • Race conditions during subscription creation
Real-dollar impact: 500 customers, 10% receive a duplicate invoice. If average MRR per customer is $100: $5,000 in duplicate charges in a single month, plus the customer trust damage.

Bug #4: Failed Payment Cascade

What is it?

A failed payment cascade triggers multiple charge attempts after an initial failure — hammering a customer's card repeatedly and burning the relationship before you even get a chance to recover the payment gracefully.

How does it happen?

  • Misconfigured invoice_settings->payment_failure_action
  • Custom retry logic layered on top of Stripe's built-in smart retries (double-retrying)
  • Missing exponential backoff in webhook retry handlers
Real-dollar impact: 20 customers with failed payments, each hit with 5+ charge attempts in 24 hours. Most cancel preemptively. At $200 ARR each: $4,000 in lost revenue — preventable with proper retry configuration.

Bug #5: Usage Metering Gaps

What is it?

Usage metering gaps occur when Stripe's metered billing doesn't receive accurate usage records — either because reports are missed, sent with wrong timestamps, or aggregated incorrectly. Customers get billed for the wrong amount.

How does it happen?

  • Usage records sent to incorrect subscription_item IDs
  • Metering events dropped silently when the usage reporter crashes
  • Incorrect action parameter (set vs. increment) causing data corruption
Real-dollar impact: 50 customers on a pay-per-GB model charged for double their actual usage. At $100 per unit overage: $5,000 in overcharges — plus the support cost when they all email you at once.

The Common Thread

Every one of these bugs has two things in common: they're hard to spot manually (they live in webhook event streams and Stripe dashboard data), and they compound silently over time. By the time you notice them, the damage is done.

The fix is automation. You need something watching your Stripe webhook stream continuously — catching anomalies the moment they appear, not when a customer files a dispute.
